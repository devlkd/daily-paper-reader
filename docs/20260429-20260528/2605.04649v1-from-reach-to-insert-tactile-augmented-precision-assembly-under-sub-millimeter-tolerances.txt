# From Reach to Insert: Tactile-Augmented Precision Assembly under Sub-Millimeter Tolerances 

Xinpan Meng, Siyao Huang, JingPu Yang, Muyuan Ma, Zhenghua Ma, Lijun Han, Gao Yuan, Houcheng Li, and Long Cheng, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract — High-precision assembly frequently involves tight-tolerance insertions, where even slight pose errors can cause jamming or excessive interaction forces, making robust and safe insertion policies difficult to obtain. This paper proposes a tactile-augmented two-stage method that combines Imitation Learning (IL) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) for precision insertion tasks. In the first stage, IL learns a reaching policy with position generalization that grasps the peg and brings it to the vicinity of the target region. In the second stage, RL executes the insertion and enables recovery from failures during contact-rich interactions. To better exploit tactile feedback, we introduce tactile group sampling to increase coverage of critical contact segments during training, and design a tactile critic to more accurately evaluate policy values, improving insertion performance while maintaining low contact forces. We conduct systematic experiments across five hole geometries and three clearance settings. Results show that our method substantially improves insertion performance across all settings; under the most challenging 0.05 mm clearance, it achieves a 67% success rate while keeping contact forces low, reducing the maximum interaction force by 60% and torque by 44%, thereby validating both effectiveness and safety for precision assembly. 

I. INTRODUCTION In industrial assembly, precision insertion under tight tol-erances is a key component of automated manufacturing, yet it remains highly challenging in real-world settings. Due to perception errors, control errors, and contact uncertainties, small discrepancies often arise between planned and executed trajectories. Under millimeter- to sub-millimeter-clearance peg-in-hole conditions, such small errors can be significantly amplified, leading to rapidly increasing interaction forces, jamming, and insertion failure. As a result, insertion success is strongly coupled with contact-force regulation, calling for safe, low-force strategies. Moreover, relying solely on vision or position feedback is often insufficient to infer contact modes and subtle misalignments, motivating the use of tactile feedback that directly reflects contact and force variations. Unlike many prior settings that learn insertion starting from a peg that is already stably grasped and initially aligned, a more realistic assembly pipeline is considered in which the robot must first grasp a randomly placed peg and then perform insertion under tight tolerances. Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into model-based methods, imitation learning (IL), and re-inforcement learning (RL). Inspired by this observation, a two-stage IL+RL method is proposed, as illustrated in Fig. 1, which decomposes the peg-in-hole task into reach and insert. In the reach stage, IL learns a grasp-and-approach policy with position generalization, reliably bringing the peg to the vicinity of the target region. II. RELATED WORK 

A. Model-based Insertion Strategies 

Early studies on insertion focused on analytic contact modeling, where force/torque equations were used to classify contact states. Visual pipelines were further incorporated to obtain pose information and estimate alignment errors for motion planning and corrective control [1], [2]. However, such approaches typically require accurate object models and contact assumptions, and are often constrained to specific ob-ject types and assembly conditions [3]. To achieve compliant and safe insertion, many methods employ variable impedance control [4] or combine visual servoing with force/impedance control [5], [6] to regulate interaction forces while main-taining alignment accuracy. Other works aim to reduce the reliance on precise perception by proposing manipulation funnel formulations [7], [8] or optimization-based contact search strategies [9]. Overall, model-based strategies are prac-tical and interpretable, but their robustness is often limited by modeling errors, manual tuning costs, and unavoidable pose/contact uncertainties in real-world settings. 

B. Learning-based Insertion Strategies 

Some works leverage deep neural networks to estimate 6D poses and contact states [10], [11], [12], [13], improving robustness but largely retaining a pipeline structure that de-pends on downstream planning and control. Imitation learning methods range from lightweight policies such as diffusion-based approaches [14], [15] to large-scale models such as VLA-style policies [16], [17], [38]. However, pure imitation learning often struggles to acquire recovery behaviors: small errors can accumulate during contact-rich interactions, easily pushing the policy into out-of-distribution (OOD) states and causing failures. C. Tactile Sensing for Contact-Rich Manipulation 

During contact, relying solely on vision or pose feedback is often insufficient to distinguish subtle misalignments and contact modes. Therefore, many works use force/torque sens-ing [5], [22] or tactile observations [26], [29] for contact-state estimation [10], [30]. Others exploit active interactions to model the environment and contact relations [11], [31], and tactile feedback is also frequently used as a core signal for high-frequency reactive control [32]. With the develop-ment of learning-based methods, an increasing number of works incorporate tactile signals as additional observations in imitation learning [33], [34] or RL policies [35], [18]. A common direction is visuo-tactile fusion: vision provides global geometry and task priors, while touch provides local contact and force details, improving alignment accuracy and robustness in contact-rich phases [36], [37]. Recent rep-resentation learning approaches further process raw tactile observations to obtain more stable tactile features, improving policy learning stability and generalization [39], [40]. III. We study a grasp-to-insert peg-in-hole assembly task on a 7-DoF Franka robot equipped with a tactile-sensing gripper. The goal is to safely insert pegs into corresponding holes with diverse hole geometries under tight clearances. At the beginning of each episode, the peg is randomly placed within a workspace region. The robot must first autonomously grasp the peg and then execute insertion under tight tolerances. Fig. 3. Overview of the proposed two-stage framework for peg-in-hole insertion. In the Reach stage, a diffusion policy generates short-horizon action sequences to move the peg near the hole entrance. In the Insert stage, Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) learns contact-rich insertion behaviors and recovery strategies. Human-in-the-loop intervention enables continuous collection of high-value interaction data, accelerating policy convergence. 

The system has access to multimodal sensory feedback in-cluding vision, tactile sensing, and robot proprioception. In the proposed asymmetric design, visual observations are used by the actor for action generation, while tactile changes are incorporated into the critic during reinforcement learning for value estimation. We denote the diametral clearance by c > 0, which charac-terizes the total difference in diameter between the hole and the peg (smaller c indicates a more challenging setting). Tight-tolerance insertion is difficult for two reasons: (i) precise alignment is required to initiate insertion, and (ii) once contact occurs, even slight pose errors can be amplified into large frictional forces and torques, leading to jamming or excessive interaction forces. To achieve safe insertion across diverse hole geometries under tight tolerances, a two-stage framework is adopted as illustrated in Fig. 3. In the Reach stage, a diffusion policy generates a short-horizon action sequence to bring the grasped peg to the vicinity of the hole entrance. In the Insert stage, Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) is utilized to learn contact-rich inser-tion behaviors, including recovery from failures. We adopt an asymmetric actor–critic design in this stage: the actor remains vision-dominant for action generation, while tactile feedback is introduced in the critic to improve value estimation during contact-rich interactions. Furthermore, human-in-the-loop in-tervention is introduced, enabling the continuous collection of high-value interaction data and accelerating convergence. 

A. Reach policy 

A dedicated reach policy πreach is trained for each hole geometry to guide the peg to the target vicinity following the grasp. The reach stage employs a diffusion policy trained via imitation learning. It takes as input the end-effector pose and multi-view image observations (comprising a wrist-mounted camera and a global camera) and outputs a short-horizon action sequence executed in a receding-horizon manner:

An asymmetric actor–critic architecture is adopted, where the actor and critic share a common ResNet visual encoder. Directly feeding such signals into the actor may increase input variance and destabilize policy learning. A. Experimental Setup 

As shown in Fig. 5, the system features a 7-DOF Franka Emika Panda robotic arm equipped with a Paxini PX-6AX GEN3 tactile sensor capable of providing high-precision six-axis force/torque measurements. For perception, a wrist-mounted Intel RealSense D405 camera enables fine-grained local observation, while a RealSense D435i camera sup-plies global scene awareness. Human demonstrations and corrective interventions are facilitated through a 3Dconnex-ion SpaceMouse interface. For computation, a workstation equipped with an NVIDIA RTX 5090 GPU and an Intel Core i9-14900 CPU is utilized for model training and real-time inference. The assembly task involves CNC-machined metal peg-in-hole workpieces with five geometries (square, round, hexagonal, L-shape, and triangular) and three clear-ances (0.05, 0.25, and 1.5 mm). B. Implementation Details 

Reach Stage Policy: For the reach stage, a diffusion policy is trained using 100 human demonstrations for each geometry. The policy is trained for 200 epochs with a batch size of 64, employing a ResNet-18 encoder to process 128 × 128

Real-Robot Fine-tuning: During the insertion stage, the policy is fine-tuned using Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) with a pixels-based observation space. An RLPD-style training pipeline is employed, sampling approximately equally from the online buffer and the demonstration buffer. To address high-precision contact challenges, an extra critic update ratio (CTA ratio) is applied, and the tactile group sampling is performed across 2 × 4 intervals of the post-grasp torques 

¯Mx and ¯My . The control frequency is maintained at 10,Hz, and zero-intervention success is typically achieved within 30 minutes of human-in-the-loop training, totaling approximately 1,000 to 2,000 environment transitions.

E. Safety and Interaction Force Analysis 

Finally, the safety of the proposed method is validated during real-world 0.05 mm insertions. We further evaluate the impact of incorporating tactile feedback on interaction dynamics. Fig. 9 illustrates the interaction force and torque profiles. While vanilla IL+RL policies achieve successful insertions, they trigger high-frequency, high-amplitude force spikes during contact, with peak normal force fluctuations exceeding 50 N and torque variations surpassing 160 N ·mm. In contrast, the proposed method regulates interaction forces within a safe range, limiting the maximum normal force fluctuation to 20 N and torque variation to 90 N ·mm, corre-sponding to reductions of 60% and 44%, respectively. By proactively detecting contact states via the tactile critic, the policy learns to suppress excessive forces, ensuring both a high success rate and operational safety in precision assembly tasks. VI. CONCLUSIONS This paper presents a tactile-augmented two-stage imita-tion and reinforcement learning framework for high-precision robotic assembly under tight tolerances. By decomposing the peg-in-hole task into a vision-guided reach stage and a tactile-responsive insert stage, the proposed method seam-lessly integrates imitation learning with real-world reinforce-ment learning. The introduction of tactile group sampling significantly improves training stability by balancing the experience replay distribution, while the tactile critic en-sures more accurate action-value estimation during contact. Real-world experiments across diverse hole geometries and clearances demonstrate that the proposed method achieves superior success rates and maintains low interaction forces. Specifically, for the most challenging 0.05 mm clearance, the system achieves a 67% success rate within only 30 minutes of human intervention, while reducing peak normal force and torque by 60% and 44%, respectively.
